Getting Around Japan: Trains, IC Cards & When a JR Pass Is Worth It

The 10-minute guide to Japanese trains: Suica on your phone, booking Shinkansen seats, luggage rules, and the honest math on rail passes.

Step 1: put Suica on your phone

Before you land, add a Suica or Pasmo card to Apple Wallet (iPhone) — it takes two minutes, needs no Japanese app, and tops up from your credit card. Tap in and out of every subway, JR line, bus and convenience store nationwide. Android users with non-Japanese phones should buy a physical Welcome Suica at the airport instead.

The honest JR Pass math

The nationwide 7-day pass costs about the same as Tokyo→Kyoto→Hiroshima→Tokyo. Rule of thumb: one-way Golden Route trips (Tokyo in, Osaka out) — skip the pass. Round trips that include Hiroshima or Kanazawa, or fast-paced multi-city loops — the pass wins. Regional passes (JR West Kansai, JR East Tohoku) are often the smarter buy.

Shinkansen seats & luggage

Non-reserved cars are fine outside holidays; book reserved seats for Golden Week, Obon and New Year. Bags over 160cm total dimensions on the Tokaido line require a (free) oversized-luggage seat reservation. Better strategy: ship suitcases between hotels with takkyubin and ride with a daypack.

Questions travelers ask

Can I use Google Maps for trains in Japan?

Yes — train routing in Google Maps is excellent in Japan, including platform numbers and fares. Locals also use the Japan Transit Planner (Jorudan) and NAVITIME apps.

Do trains really stop at midnight?

Mostly yes — last trains leave city centers around 00:00–00:30 and restart near 5:00. Miss it and your options are a taxi, a capsule hotel, or karaoke until dawn.
