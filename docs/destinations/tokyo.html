東Kanto

Tokyo

Neon canyons, quiet shrines and the best food city on Earth

Best time to visit: Mar–May, Oct–Nov

Tokyo is less a single city than a constellation of villages stitched together by the world's most punctual trains. In one afternoon you can watch the crossing at Shibuya, drink hand-poured coffee in a backstreet kissaten, and stand in near silence at Meiji Shrine's forested grounds. The city rewards walkers: neighborhoods like Yanaka, Shimokitazawa and Kagurazaka feel decades and worlds apart from the skyscrapers of Shinjuku.

Food is the real headline. Tokyo has more Michelin stars than any city on the planet, but its genius lives in ¥1,000 bowls of ramen, standing sushi bars, and department-store food halls. Base yourself near the Yamanote Line loop and the whole city opens up.

Through the seasons

Cherry blossoms turn Ueno Park, Meguro River and Chidorigafuchi into pink tunnels late March–early April; picnic (hanami) under the trees like locals.

Hot and humid, but festival season: Sumida River fireworks in July, rooftop beer gardens, and day trips to breezy Enoshima or Okutama.

Late November ginkgo avenues glow gold at Meiji Jingu Gaien and Icho Namiki; crisp skies make it the best season for Shibuya Sky views.

Dry, sunny and crowd-light. Illuminations at Marunouchi and Yebisu Garden Place, New Year shrine visits, and the year's clearest Mt. Fuji views from observation decks.

In pictures

Don't miss

  • Shibuya Crossing & Shibuya Sky observation deck
  • Senso-ji Temple and old-town Asakusa
  • Meiji Shrine's forest and Harajuku's Takeshita Street
  • Tsukiji Outer Market breakfast crawl
  • Day trip to teamLab Planets or Ghibli Museum (book ahead)

Getting there

From Narita Airport: Skyliner or Narita Express, ~40–60 min. From Haneda: monorail or Keikyu line, ~20–30 min to central Tokyo.
