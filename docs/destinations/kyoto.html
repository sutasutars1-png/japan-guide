京Kansai

Kyoto

A thousand years of temples, tea houses and lantern-lit lanes

Best time to visit: Mar–Apr (sakura), Nov (autumn leaves)

Japan's imperial capital for over a millennium, Kyoto holds seventeen UNESCO World Heritage sites and roughly two thousand temples and shrines. The famous ones — Fushimi Inari's vermilion gates, Kinkaku-ji's golden pavilion, the bamboo grove of Arashiyama — earn their crowds, but Kyoto's magic is strongest at 7 a.m., before the tour buses arrive, or in lesser-known temples where you may sit alone with a garden.

Stay one night in a machiya townhouse or ryokan, eat a kaiseki dinner or a humble obanzai set, and walk the Gion district at dusk when lanterns come on. Rent a bicycle: the city is flat and compact.

Through the seasons

Sakura along the Philosopher's Path and Maruyama Park's weeping cherry are iconic — book lodging months ahead and start sightseeing by 7 a.m.

Humid, but July's month-long Gion Matsuri (grand processions July 17 & 24) and riverside kawadoko dining on the Kamo River make it worth sweating.

Mid-to-late November is peak koyo: Tofuku-ji and Eikando maples are spectacular, and several temples open for evening illuminations.

The secret season — Kinkaku-ji under fresh snow is a once-in-a-trip photo, temples are quiet, and yudofu (tofu hot pot) tastes best in the cold.

In pictures

Don't miss

  • Fushimi Inari Shrine at sunrise (free, open 24h)
  • Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion) and Ryoan-ji rock garden
  • Arashiyama bamboo grove + Tenryu-ji temple
  • Gion at dusk and the Philosopher's Path
  • Nishiki Market food street

Getting there

From Tokyo: Tokaido Shinkansen, ~2h15m. From Kansai Airport: JR Haruka express, ~75 min.
